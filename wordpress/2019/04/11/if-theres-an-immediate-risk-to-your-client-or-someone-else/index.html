If there’s an immediate risk to your client or someone else

It’s ok to call the police or an ambulance in an emergency – you won’t be breaching confidentiality. You shouldn’t need to give any details about why the client was at a local Citizen’s Advice – just their name and address.

If you need to call the police, tell the client if it’s safe to do so – don’t put yourself at risk. Also tell them you might need to give the police their name and address.

You should also warn other clients if you’re calling the police – give them the chance to leave before the police arrive.

You’ll need to explain our policy on confidentiality to the police – explain that you won’t give details of any other clients.

Check if your chief officer can authorise a breach of confidentiality

Your chief officer can authorise a breach of confidentiality if someone is at immediate risk, or if a child or vulnerable adult are involved. You can read more about what to do if a child or vulnerable adult are at risk in our safeguarding guidance.

There could be an immediate risk if a client:

  • threatens to harm themselves or someone else
  • threatens to take their own life
  • takes an overdose
  • is suddenly taken ill, for example if they’re unconscious or you think they’re having a heart attack

If a client’s behaviour makes you concerned for their immediate safety or the safety of others, you should take immediate action. Give as much information as you can to help your chief officer make a decision quickly.

You’ll need to give evidence to show that:

  • someone’s health, safety or wellbeing is in immediate danger – for example a client, a member of their family, or an adviser
  • someone needs urgent help, for example from the police or medical professionals
