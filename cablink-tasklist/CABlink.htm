Maternity leave

28 May 2019

When you have a baby or adopt a child you’re entitled to take time off work.

You can use the leave planner for parents on GOV.UK to work out:

  • how much maternity leave you can take
  • when you can start your maternity leave
  • how much statutory maternity pay you might get
  • if your partner can take paternity leave
  • if you and your partner can take shared parental leave

Work out what you and your partner are entitled to on GOV.UK.

Telling your line manager

Talk to your line manager about your pregnancy as soon as you feel ready. You might want to wait until after your first scan.

You have to tell your line manager at least 15 weeks before your due date.

If you haven’t told your line manager about your pregnancy you’ll need to take annual leave to go to any antenatal appointments.

When you tell your line manager you should also talk to them about:

  • when you think you might start maternity leave
  • whether you think you’ll come back to work after maternity leave

You don’t have to decide these things straight away but it’s good to start talking to your line manager about them as soon as you can.

Once you’ve told your line manager about your pregnancy, you’ll need to fill in a risk assessment form. This is to make sure you have what you need when you’re at work during your pregnancy and deal with any health and safety concerns.

Make a copy of the pregnant worker risk assessment form , fill it in and send it to facilitieshelpdesk@citizensadvice.org.uk

Your manager should check you’ve completed this form and the assessment has been done.

You can check your rights as a pregnant employee on GOV.UK

Planning your maternity leave dates

The earliest you can go on maternity leave is usually 11 weeks before your due date. At one of your early appointments, usually around 20 weeks, you’ll be given a MATB1 form by your midwife. This will confirm your expected due date.

If your baby comes early or you’re off sick with a pregnancy-related illness in the last 4 weeks of your pregnancy, Citizens Advice will automatically start your maternity leave early.

If you want to come back after maternity leave

You need to say on the family leave form that you plan to return to work. This gives you the right to come back at any time up to the end of your maternity leave to the same job:

  • in the same place
  • with the same terms of your original contract

You can change the date you want to come back as long as you tell your line manager at least 8 weeks before the date you originally agreed.

If you don’t want to come back after maternity leave

You need to say this on the family leave form. If you’re not coming back you’ll only get statutory maternity pay while you’re off. You won’t be entitled to Citizens Advice maternity pay.

Telling the People team

When you’ve got your MATB1 form and decided on your maternity leave dates, fill in the family leave form . You’ll need to upload a copy of your MATB1 form to include with it.

When the People team have the completed form, they’ll send you confirmation of the maternity pay you’re entitled to.

Maternity pay

Most employees will be able to get statutory maternity pay (SMP). You can work out how much statutory maternity pay you can get using the maternity planner on GOV.UK.

Citizens Advice maternity pay

This is 26 weeks’ full pay. You can get Citizens Advice maternity pay if:

  • 15 weeks before your due date you’ve worked at Citizens Advice for at least a year
  • 11 weeks before your due date you’re still working at Citizens Advice
  • you plan to come back to work for at least 6 months after your maternity leave finishes
  • you give Citizens Advice your MATB1 form showing your due date at least 21 days before your maternity leave is due to start

If you get Citizens Advice maternity pay your SMP will be included in the total amount you get each month. When your Citizens Advice maternity pay finishes your SMP will carry on if you’re still entitled to it.

Your maternity pay starts when your maternity leave starts.

If you’re on a fixed term contract

If your contract ends more than 15 weeks before your due date, you won’t get Citizens Advice maternity pay.

If your contract ends any time after that, you’ll get Citizens Advice maternity pay up to the date your contract finishes. Your Citizens Advice maternity leave will also finish on this date. If you’re still entitled to SMP, you’ll continue to get it after your contract has finished.

If you’re not sure what maternity pay you can get with your current contract, email payroll.changes@citizensadvice.org.uk

If you’re not entitled to maternity pay

If you can’t get statutory maternity pay or Citizens Advice maternity pay you might be able to get maternity allowance.

The People team will give you a completed SMP1 form after they’ve received your family leave form. The SMP1 form will confirm you can’t get statutory maternity pay and explain what to do next. You’ll need the SMP1 form and your MATB1 form to apply for maternity allowance.

Check if you can get maternity allowance and how to apply on GOV.UK

Time off for appointments

You can take paid time off to go to antenatal and postnatal appointments if:

  • they fall during your working hours
  • you’ve already told the People team you’re pregnant

It doesn’t matter how long you’ve worked at Citizens Advice or if you’re full time or part time.

When you book an appointment, ask for an appointment card and a certificate from your GP, midwife or health visitor showing when the appointment is. You might need to show this to your line manager when you ask for the time off. You don’t need to show any evidence for your first appointment.

If you’re ill while you’re pregnant

If you’re off sick with a pregnancy-related illness in the 4 weeks before your due date, this will automatically start your maternity leave early.

If you’re off sick more than 4 weeks before your due date, it will count as sick leave and you’ll need to follow the usual processes for reporting sickness. Check what to do if you’re off sick.

You can’t get sick pay once you’re on maternity leave.

Working while you’re on maternity leave

You can work for up to 10 days during your maternity leave. These are called keeping in touch (KIT) days. You can use them for catching up on the work your team have been doing, attending training or any other meetings.

Your 10 KIT days:

  • can be taken as full or half days
  • can be taken at any time, except in the 2 weeks after you give birth
  • don’t have to be taken one after the other
  • will be paid at your normal rate

Talk to your line manager about when you’d like to take your KIT days. You don’t have to plan these before you go on maternity leave and you don’t have to use all 10 days.

If you choose not to use any KIT days your line manager will contact you while you’re on maternity leave to plan your return to work.

Check your rights when you’re on maternity leave on GOV.UK

Coming back to work after maternity leave

If you want to ask for flexible working hours, you’ll need to make a flexible working request. Talk to your line manager about this and any other adjustments you think you might need when you’re planning your return to work.

If you’ve been made redundant while on maternity leave, you should legally be offered another suitable job. You have priority for this over other staff not on maternity leave. Check your rights as a pregnant employee on GOV.UK

If you decide not to go back to work or go back for less than 6 months

You need to email or write to your line manager to give written notice that you’re not going to go back. It’s a good idea to tell your line manager as soon as you can to avoid getting any maternity pay that you’ll have to pay back later. Check your contract for how much notice you have to give.

If you’re already receiving Citizens Advice maternity pay, it will stop at the end of your notice period. You’ll have to pay back the CA maternity pay you’ve been paid, minus any SMP you were entitled to. You’ll also have to do this if you go back to work but leave after less than 6 months.

You’ll still get the rest of the SMP you’re entitled to.

Maternity leave and annual leave

You’ll build up annual leave at the same rate as usual while you’re on maternity leave. If any Citizens Advice days or bank holidays fall during your maternity leave, they’ll be added to your annual leave total and you can use them when you go back to work.

If your baby arrives when you’re on annual leave before your maternity leave starts, you have to start your maternity leave early and use the annual leave later in the year.

If you’ve carried over any annual leave from the previous year, you should add this onto the end of your maternity leave and take it before you come back to work.

Maternity leave and your pension

If you’re a member of the group pension scheme, Citizens Advice will still contribute 6.5% of your full salary while you’re getting Citizens Advice maternity pay or statutory maternity pay.

While you are on full pay, your own contributions will continue as normal.

When you move to SMP only, your own contributions will change to a percentage of your SMP rather than your full salary. Your own contributions will be taken from your maternity pay.

Citizens Advice won’t make any contributions to your pension after your SMP finishes.

If you don’t want to make pension contributions while you’re on maternity leave, email staffpensionforms@citizensadvice.org.uk to tell the People team.
